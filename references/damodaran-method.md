# Damodaran Method

This reference condenses the reusable valuation method. Use it as a checklist while valuing a project or company.

## Mental Model

Damodaran's valuation approach is pragmatic:

- Every asset can be valued, but some valuations are noisier than others.
- A valuation is a bridge between a story and numbers.
- Models do not value companies; analysts do.
- The process is as useful as the final value because it reveals what drives value.
- Uncertainty is not a reason to abandon valuation; it is a reason to be explicit.

## Value Drivers

Ask four questions:

1. What cash flows do existing assets or committed operations generate?
2. What future growth can the business earn, and what reinvestment is needed to create it?
3. How risky are the cash flows, and what discount rate or required return matches that risk?
4. When does the asset become mature, and what does stable growth look like?

## Narrative To Numbers

Turn the business story into assumptions:

- Market story -> revenue growth, market share, pricing, volume, churn, or utilization.
- Competitive advantage -> target margin, ROIC, fade period, and persistence of excess returns.
- Business model -> fixed/variable cost structure, operating leverage, capex, working capital, R&D, and taxes.
- Financing story -> target debt ratio, cost of debt, default risk, dilution, and claims seniority.
- Macro story -> currency, inflation, risk-free rate, ERP, country risk, commodity cycle, or exchange rates.
- Life-cycle story -> length of high growth, margin convergence, reinvestment fade, and terminal assumptions.

If the story cannot be translated into drivers, say what is missing.

## Model Selection

Use the simplest model that captures the economics:

- FCFF/WACC: default for operating companies and projects.
- FCFE: useful when direct equity cash flows are forecastable and leverage is stable.
- Dividend discount: useful for firms where dividends approximate available equity cash flow.
- Excess return: useful for financial service firms where debt and reinvestment are hard to define.
- APV: useful when financing choices, tax shields, or changing leverage are central.
- Sum-of-the-parts: useful when businesses have different margins, risks, growth, or currencies.
- Real options: useful only for exclusivity plus uncertainty plus active managerial choice.
- Liquidation/distress/equity option: useful when survival is uncertain or debt dominates equity value.

## Input Estimation

Cash flow:

- Start from operating income or project operating profit.
- Normalize unusual items and accounting distortions.
- Apply taxes consistently.
- Subtract reinvestment: capex, acquisitions, working capital, R&D capitalization, or sales-to-capital needs.
- Separate operating assets from cash, securities, cross-holdings, and other non-operating assets.

Growth:

- Tie revenue growth to market size, share, pricing, and capacity.
- Tie earnings growth to margins and operating leverage.
- Tie value-creating growth to reinvestment and return on capital.
- Do not use terminal growth above the long-run nominal growth of the economy in the valuation currency.

Risk and discount rate:

- Choose currency first.
- Use a risk-free rate in that currency.
- Estimate ERP and country risk premiums from current sources when material.
- Estimate business risk from operations, not just regression beta.
- Relever beta using target or evolving leverage.
- Estimate cost of debt from default risk and current borrowing conditions.
- Match discount rate to cash flow type and currency.

Terminal value:

- Terminal year must look mature: stable growth, stable margins, stable reinvestment, stable leverage, stable risk.
- Use terminal reinvestment consistent with growth and ROIC.
- If the business will decline, use a declining or finite-life model instead of forcing positive perpetuity growth.
- Diagnose terminal value share of total value. A high share is not automatically wrong, but it demands tighter explanation.

## Pricing Cross-Check

Use multiples as a pricing check with four tests:

1. Definition test: numerator, denominator, lease/debt/cash treatment, time period, and accounting basis are consistent.
2. Description test: understand the distribution, median, outliers, and sample size.
3. Analytical test: link the multiple to fundamentals such as growth, margin, reinvestment, ROIC, payout, and risk.
4. Application test: choose peers with similar economics, not just the same label.

Reconcile differences between value and price by stating the market-implied story.

## Uncertainty

Use three layers:

- Sensitivity: one or two variables at a time.
- Scenarios: coherent alternative stories, not random input changes.
- Simulation: use only when distributions are defensible and the output helps a decision.

Report what would change the conclusion.

## Special Situation Routing

- Young/startup: focus on total market, adoption, unit economics, target margins, reinvestment, survival probability, and dilution.
- High growth: test scalability of revenue, margin path, reinvestment needs, risk fade, and employee options.
- Mature: focus on efficiency, capital structure, acquisitions, control, and payout.
- Declining: model shrinkage, asset sales, distress, pensions, litigation, and liquidation alternatives.
- Cyclical/commodity: normalize across the cycle; avoid valuing peak earnings as permanent.
- Financial services: focus on book equity, regulatory capital, ROE, cost of equity, dividends/buybacks, and excess returns.
- Intangible-heavy: capitalize R&D or other long-lived operating investments when needed.
- Emerging market: handle currency and country risk explicitly; avoid double-counting.
- User/subscriber businesses: value users from unit economics, retention, CAC, margin, and growth, then reconcile to firm value.

## Common Failure Modes

- Choosing a model before understanding the business.
- Mixing currencies, inflation assumptions, or cash flow types.
- Using high growth without reinvestment.
- Letting terminal value carry an impossible story.
- Treating beta as pure truth instead of a noisy risk estimate.
- Applying an arbitrary size/liquidity/country premium without explaining where risk is already captured.
- Using peer multiples without checking definitions and fundamentals.
- Presenting a point estimate when the honest answer is a range.
